The Challenge

20.4% of customers churn, and the two errors are unequal: missing a churner loses the whole relationship, a false alarm costs one retention offer.

The Solution

Four classifiers tuned under one protocol; XGBoost leads on both ranking metrics, then the decision threshold is set to the F2-optimal 0.23.

The Value

Catches about 432 of 509 test-set churners (85%); as long as a churner is worth more than 3.3 retention offers, the aggressive operating point is cheaper.

00 Executive Summary

Executive Summary

A machine learning analysis of 10,000 retail banking customers: who leaves, why, and what the bank can do about it before it happens. The final model is XGBoost with SMOTE, tuned by 5-fold grid search.

Four findings

  • Age is the strongest predictor. Both EDA and SHAP put age first: churn risk rises sharply between 40 and 50; the median churned customer is around 45, the median retained around 35.
  • Inactivity precedes churn. IsActiveMember=0 is a top-three SHAP driver and the most usable early-warning signal — falling engagement shows up well before the account closes.
  • More products, more churn. Customers holding 3 or 4 products churn more, not less (counterintuitive); product count ranks second in all three importance views.
  • Germany is different. German customers churn far more often than French or Spanish ones, in both raw counts and model rankings.

01 Data & EDA

Data & Exploration

The dataset holds demographic attributes (age, gender, country), account attributes (balance, product count, credit score, tenure, card ownership, activity flag) and the churn label. Quality checks found no missing values and no multicollinearity; identifier columns were dropped. Preprocessing: 75/25 stratified split (random_state=42 throughout), one-hot for Geography, ordinal for Gender, standardized numerics — encoders and scaler fitted on the training set only, avoiding leakage.

02 Model Bake-off

Model Comparison

Four classifiers were tuned under one protocol so their comparison is like for like: each sits in an imblearn pipeline that applies SMOTE to the training folds only, and each was tuned by 5-fold GridSearchCV scoring ROC-AUC.

Model Accuracy Precision Recall F1 ROC-AUC PR-AUC
XGBoost with SMOTE 0.8544 0.6507 0.6149 0.6323 0.8757 0.7265
Random forest 0.8440 0.6042 0.6778 0.6389 0.8681 0.7002
KNN 0.7388 0.4184 0.7250 0.5306 0.8078 0.5277
Logistic regression 0.7160 0.3945 0.7387 0.5144 0.7900 0.4801

Precision, recall and F1 are computed at the default 0.5 threshold; ROC-AUC and PR-AUC measure ranking quality independent of any threshold. With 20% churners, a no-skill model scores a PR-AUC near 0.20, so PR-AUC separates the models more sharply than ROC-AUC.

XGBoost leads on both ranking metrics and is the recommended model. Logistic regression and KNN post high recall only by flagging large numbers of loyal customers: at a precision near 0.4, six out of ten contacts would be wasted. Random forest comes close and slightly beats XGBoost on recall and F1 at the 0.5 threshold, but a threshold can be adjusted after training, while ranking quality cannot.

Metric · Why accuracy is not the headline

Accuracy was rejected as a tuning metric because a model that always predicts "stays" is already 79.6% accurate on this data — the metric has no discriminative power on imbalanced data. Reporting ranking metrics (ROC-AUC / PR-AUC) plus an explicit operating point reflects the true ability to separate the rare class.

03 Threshold Tuning

Threshold Tuning

The default 0.5 threshold treats both error types as equally costly. They are not: a missed churner costs the bank the customer relationship, a false alarm costs one retention offer. Sweeping the threshold and maximizing F2 (recall weighted twice as heavily as precision) gives an operating point that matches the retention objective:

Operating point Threshold Precision Recall F1 F2 Flagged
Default 0.50 0.6507 0.6149 0.6323 0.6218 481
F2-optimal 0.23 0.4337 0.8487 0.5741 0.7124 996

At 0.23 the model catches about 432 of the 509 test-set churners instead of about 313, and the contact list roughly doubles. Whether that trade pays off depends on offer cost against customer value; when campaign capacity is fixed, the threshold can sit anywhere on the precision-recall curve — which is exactly why the model with the strongest ranking is chosen.

04 Break-even Analysis

Break-even Cost Analysis

F2 is a proxy for "recall matters more"; the real objective is cost. This dataset has no cost fields (customer value, offer cost, campaign success rate), so rather than invent them, the analysis assumes only the cost ratio C = cost of a missed churner / cost of a wasted retention offer, and compares the two error types. At the default 0.5 threshold the model makes 168 wasted offers (false positives) and 196 missed churners (false negatives); at the tuned 0.23 threshold, 564 wasted offers and 77 missed churners. Setting the two error costs equal gives a break-even ratio of about C* = 3.3: the tuned threshold has lower total cost whenever missing a churner costs more than roughly 3.3 wasted offers.

Insight · The conclusion holds for any bank respecting the ratio

In retail banking a lost customer relationship is worth many times a single retention offer, so any realistic ratio sits well above 3.3 and the aggressive operating point is justified. No currency figure is assumed, so the conclusion holds for any bank whose economics respect the ratio. (SMOTE distorts probability calibration, so the operating threshold is found empirically rather than from the textbook 1/(1+C) rule.)

05 Why Customers Leave

Why Customers Leave: SHAP Attribution

SHAP assigns each feature a signed contribution to each prediction, so the model's reasoning can be read globally and per customer. To guard against method artifacts, the SHAP ranking was cross-checked against random forest impurity importances and XGBoost gain importances; the same features top all three.

The two test-set extremes illustrate how the drivers combine. The highest-risk customer (churn probability 0.998) stacks every driver: 4 products, an age well above average, inactive, an above-average balance, located in Germany; only a short tenure pulls the other way, far too weakly to matter. The lowest-risk customer (churn probability 0.005) is the mirror image: 2 products, young, active, zero balance, high credit score.

06 Action Plan

Action Plan

Initiative What to do Basis in the analysis
Monthly risk scoring Score every customer with XGBoost at the tuned threshold; give service teams each customer's top three SHAP factors before a call. Best ranking quality of the four candidates; 85% of churners caught at the tuned threshold.
Re-engagement campaign Target inactive members in the medium-to-high risk band with a bonus rate, a fee waiver or a consultation. Inactivity is a top-three SHAP driver.
Portfolio review Contact customers holding 3+ products for a simplification review; reward sales on fitting combinations rather than product count. Product count ranks second in all three importance views.
German market review Run competitor analysis and collect customer and branch feedback in Germany. Germany shows elevated churn in the EDA and the tree-model rankings.

Follow-ups & Reproducibility

Follow-ups: A/B-test the offers against a control group, retrain quarterly, recheck the threshold whenever campaign economics change, and surface the risk score plus SHAP factors in the CRM. The notebook runs end to end locally (uv run jupyter nbconvert --to notebook --execute); all random seeds are fixed at 42, and figures are written to figures/.
