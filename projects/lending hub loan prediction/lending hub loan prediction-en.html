LendingClub Loan Default: A Threshold Decision under Asymmetric Cost

A missed default costs 4.2 times a wrongly declined loan, so the model is tuned to lose less money, not to make fewer classification errors. The tuned LightGBM cuts expected loss 26.5% against approving everyone — this analysis checks how much of that number survives scrutiny.

The Challenge

16.8% of loans charge off, and the two errors cost very differently: a missed default loses $9,437 of principal, a wrongly declined good loan forgoes $2,243 of interest — a 4.2:1 ratio. A model optimizing accuracy answers the wrong question.

The Approach

Three models compared under one leak-free protocol; LightGBM is chosen on lowest out-of-fold cost, with the decision threshold set to 0.16 for the cost asymmetry rather than the default 0.5.

The Value

Catches 74.9% of defaults, total cost $2.63M against $3.58M for approving everyone — 26.5% lower. But a single 60-month term rule takes most of that reduction; the model's margin is default coverage and within-book ranking.

00 Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Of the 9,004 completed LendingClub loans in this extract, 16.8% ended in charge-off, and the two ways an underwriting model can be wrong carry different prices: a missed default loses $9,437 of principal on average, a wrongly declined good loan forgoes $2,243 of interest, a 4.2:1 asymmetry measured directly from the repayment records. Three classifiers were trained under one protocol, and LightGBM was selected on lowest out-of-fold cost — its value lies not in a leaderboard score but in losing the least money.

Four findings

  • Loan term is the single strongest lever. 60-month loans default at 32.4%, three times the 36-month rate of 11.3%; term also tops the SHAP ranking.
  • A one-variable term rule captures 24.5 of the model's 26.5 points of saving. Declining all 60-month terms already saves 24.5%; the model buys default coverage (74.9% against 48.0%) and within-book ranking.
  • Holding 74.9% recall sends 46.9% of applications to manual review. The recall floor is a policy dial priced in queue capacity; a 0.20 threshold cuts the queue to 37.1% at 63.3% recall.
  • The saving depends on the accounting. Crediting the interest defaulters pay before charging off drops the reduction from a headline 26.5% to 6.6% — identical flag list, and the conservative net figure is the one to quote.

01 Data & Cost Calibration

Data & Cost Calibration

The dataset is a cleaned extract of LendingClub public lending data: 9,004 completed loans issued between 2012 and 2015, 29 columns. Every loan is finished business, either Fully Paid or Charged Off; that closure is what makes the cost calibration possible. Cleaning needs are minimal: no duplicate rows, 319 missing cells (0.1%) confined to employment length and revolving utilization.

The data also holds repayment-outcome columns (total_pymnt, total_rec_prncp, total_rec_int, out_prncp). Recorded during or after repayment and unknown at approval, they do exactly one job — calibrating the costs — and are removed before any feature reaches a model.

Cost Calibration · Costs come from the data, not an assumption

A missed default (false negative) costs the unrecovered principal of charged-off loans, funded_amnt − total_rec_prncp, at $9,437. Using the average funded amount ($13,663) would overstate the loss by $4,227, because borrowers repay part of the principal before charging off.

A wrongly declined good loan (false positive) costs the mean interest collected on fully-paid loans, total_rec_int, at $2,243.

The resulting 4.2:1 cost ratio is exactly what pulls the optimal threshold far below 0.5.

02 Risk Factors

Risk Factors

Every numeric feature except open_acc separates default from repayment at high significance (p < 0.001), but significance is cheap on 9,000 rows, so the useful filter is effect size. By Cohen's d, interest rate is the one large effect, revolving utilization is moderate, and the rest are small — open accounts carry no univariate signal.

Feature Mean, fully paid Mean, charged off Cohen's d
Interest rate 11.63 14.59 0.73
Revolving utilization 50.94 59.44 0.31
Loan amount 11,967 13,895 0.23
Annual income 71,110 62,012 −0.19
Inquiries, last 6 months 0.82 1.02 0.19
Debt-to-income 13.73 14.70 0.15

The categorical breakdown is clearer than any single number. Credit grade tracks default monotonically, from 7.4% at grade A to 52.9% at grade G, and loan term is the sharpest split in the data: 60-month loans default at 32.4%, against 11.3% for 36-month.

Counterintuitive · Verified income runs riskier

Income verification runs backwards: verified borrowers default more (19.4%) than unverified ones (14.4%). Because verification is demanded of applicants who already look risky, the field records suspicion, not causation — it cannot be read as a clearance signal. A textbook selection effect.

Banding the continuous drivers shows monotone relationships, not thresholds. Default rises with revolving utilization from 10.5% (below 20%) to 23.7% (above 80%) with no dip in the middle, so there is no protective "moderate utilization" band. Income runs the opposite way, from 20.5% under $40K to 11.1% above $100K.

03 Where Losses Concentrate

Where Losses Concentrate

Counting defaults sharpens the policy picture more than the default rate does: grades E through G hold 9.4% of loans but produce 22.0% of defaults, a thin and expensive tail. Tightening policy on it cuts losses disproportionately. Geographically, with states under 50 loans set aside, Nevada runs hottest at 27.4% against the 16.8% portfolio rate.

04 Model Bake-off

Three Models under One Protocol

Logistic regression, random forest, and LightGBM run the same protocol: outcome columns removed; a 75/25 stratified split; high-cardinality categoricals (addr_state, purpose) via cross-fitted target encoders learned from training folds only; SMOTE inside the pipeline so synthetic samples never leak into validation; hyperparameters via grid search on average precision. Out-of-fold (OOF) predictions from 5-fold CV both feed the threshold tuning and, against test scores, measure overfitting — test edges out OOF for every model, so nothing memorized its folds.

Model Threshold Recall Precision F2 ROC-AUC PR-AUC Test cost
LightGBM 0.16 74.9% 26.9% 0.552 0.733 0.372 $2,627,872
Logistic regression 0.27 77.0% 26.4% 0.557 0.744 0.387 $2,648,817
Random forest 0.19 79.9% 25.4% 0.559 0.733 0.372 $2,715,463

Recall, precision, and F2 are at each model's cost-optimal threshold; ROC-AUC and PR-AUC are threshold-free. PR-AUC of about 0.37 is a roughly 2.2x lift over the 0.168 no-skill baseline, the realistic level for application-time information alone. Logistic regression leads every ranking column.

Model Selection · The two models are statistically interchangeable

Logistic regression leads on ranking, random forest on recall, LightGBM on cost and precision, and the three costs differ by under 1%. Under a bootstrap, the $20,134 mean cost gap to logistic regression carries an interval of [−$121,482, +$156,074], and LightGBM is cheaper in only 60.2% of resamples — on cost the two are statistically interchangeable, and the choice is operational. LightGBM is chosen on lowest OOF cost; logistic regression is a defensible interpretable alternative.

Ablation · SMOTE is not load-bearing

At a 16.8% positive rate, resampling is not load-bearing: refitting each tuned model with the SMOTE step removed, hyperparameters unchanged, moves OOF metrics by at most 0.010, in no consistent direction (random forest PR-AUC 0.337→0.347, LightGBM 0.342→0.337, logistic regression flat). The pipeline's adaptation to imbalance happens at the decision threshold; SMOTE stays only because the hyperparameters were tuned with it in place.

05 Threshold as a Business Decision

Setting the Threshold as a Business Decision

The default 0.5 threshold minimizes classification errors, but the two errors are not equally costly. For each model, thresholds from 0.05 to 0.94 are scored on the OOF predictions with the calibrated cost function (FN × $9,437 + FP × $2,243), subject to catching at least 75% of defaults — a default-prediction system that misses over a quarter of defaults is not doing its job. The model is picked on lowest OOF cost; the test set plays no part in selection and is read once.

At threshold 0.16, LightGBM catches 74.9% of test defaults at 26.9% precision for a total error cost of $2,627,872. Approving everyone costs $3,576,469, so the model cuts expected loss 26.5%. The low precision is deliberate: at 4.2:1, flagging three good loans to catch one default is the cheaper mistake — flagged loans are routed to a manual-review queue, never to an automatic decline.

Uncertainty · Bootstrap 95% CI (×2,000)

Every headline number comes from one 2,251-loan test set; a bootstrap (2,000 resamples of the fixed predictions, no refitting) puts a width on each: ROC-AUC 0.733 [0.706, 0.760]; PR-AUC 0.372 [0.325, 0.422]; recall 74.9% [70.5%, 79.2%]; precision 26.9% [24.3%, 29.5%]; cost reduction vs approve-all 26.5% [18.8%, 33.2%]. None is narrow; none crosses a level that would change the decision.

06 Rule Baselines & Review Burden

Rule Baselines & Review Burden

Approving everyone is the baseline the 26.5% is measured against, and no lender operates that way. The fairer comparison is a policy rule an underwriter could apply with no model: decline grades E–G, or decline 60-month terms. Scored with the same cost function on the same test set, the term rule alone recovers most of what the model recovers.

Policy Flag rate Recall Test cost Reduction
Approve everyone 0.0% 0.0% $3,576,469 0.0%
Decline grades E–G 9.2% 21.9% $3,071,332 14.1%
Decline 60-month terms 24.7% 48.0% $2,700,037 24.5%
LightGBM (threshold 0.16) 46.9% 74.9% $2,627,872 26.5%

24.5 percentage points: what the model buys beyond the term rule is 2 points of cost, 26.9 points of recall (74.9% against 48.0%), and the ability to rank risk inside the 60-month book instead of declining the product line wholesale. A lender that judges only by this cost function can defend the simple rule; one that wants default coverage, or wants to keep writing 60-month loans, needs the ranking.

Flagged loans go to human reviewers, so the flag rate is an operating cost in its own right: 1,056 of 2,251 test applications, 46.9%, flagged to catch 74.9% of defaults. Nearly half the intake lands on the review desk, workable only if review is cheap or heavily assisted — the direct price of the 0.75 recall floor.

Threshold Flag rate Recall
0.16 46.9% 74.9%

Row one is the test-set operating point; the rest are out-of-fold readings.

Caveat · When review capacity binds, this table sets the threshold

The 75% recall floor is a policy input, not an estimate, and this curve is where any renegotiation starts. If review capacity is the binding constraint, the threshold gets set from this table, not the cost function.

07 Why Loans Default

Why Loans Default: SHAP Attribution

A score an underwriter cannot explain does not survive contact with a declined applicant, so the selected LightGBM is attributed with SHAP on original-unit features. Ranked by mean absolute SHAP: loan term 0.453, annual income 0.251, grade 0.211, recent inquiries 0.179, purpose 0.157. Two of the four engineered features rank in the top eight: the grade-term interaction (0.142) and the financial stress index (0.111).

Direction Check · Mean signed SHAP misleads

Interest rate raises risk. Its mean signed SHAP is almost exactly zero, which reads as "no effect", but that statistic hides direction. The dependence plot says otherwise: the correlation between the rate and its own SHAP value is +0.55, higher rates push predicted risk up, matching the d = 0.73 gap in section 02; positive pushes on high-rate loans and negative pushes on low-rate loans cancel in the mean.

Utilization has no protective middle band. It pushes risk up across its whole range, matching the no-protective-band shape. Income (−0.83) and grade (+0.91) behave as expected, and the stress index runs positive (+0.75).

08 Actions & Limits

Actions & Limits

Initiative What to do Basis in the analysis
Score at the cost-optimal threshold Run new applications through LightGBM at 0.16; route flagged loans to manual review, not an automatic decline. Catches 74.9% of defaults, cutting expected loss 26.5% below approve-all (6.6% net).
Reprice or cap long-term loans Add a risk premium to 60-month loans, or restrict them to higher grades. 60-month terms default at 32.4% vs 11.3% for 36-month; term is the top SHAP driver.
Tighten the low-grade tail Add review or hard cut-offs for grades E through G. 9.4% of loans, but 22.0% of defaults.
Do not treat verification as clearance Do not relax scrutiny because income is verified. Verified loans default more (19.4% vs 14.4%) — a selection effect.

Every dollar figure rests on one calibration choice: a missed default counted as $9,437 of lost principal. Defaulters also pay interest before charging off, and crediting it puts the net FN cost at $6,684, moving the ratio from 4.2:1 to 3.0:1. Re-deriving the decision under the net accounting changes nothing about who gets flagged — the optimal threshold stays at 0.16 — but the claimed saving falls from 26.5% to 6.6%.

Accounting FN cost Ratio Optimal threshold Test reduction
Headline: unrecovered principal $9,437 4.2:1 0.16 26.5%
Net: principal minus interest received $6,684 3.0:1 0.16 6.6%
Limits

This is a single cross-section of completed loans with no origination dates: no time-based validation, and credit_history_years is a fixed-reference approximation. Interest rate embeds LendingClub's own risk pricing, so part of its predictive power restates information the platform encoded at origination rather than an independent borrower signal. The cost figures use portfolio-average FN/FP costs (accounting sensitivity above); a production system would price each loan's exposure individually. An ROC-AUC of 0.73 to 0.74 is the realistic ceiling for application-time data alone on this portfolio; claims of much more from similar data deserve suspicion of leakage.
