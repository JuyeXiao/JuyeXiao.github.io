First-Transaction Fraud Detection: A Recall Ceiling and a Threshold Artifact

The Challenge

A 1.02% imbalance (1,415 / 138,376) makes accuracy useless — predicting "legitimate" for everyone scores 98.98% and catches zero fraud. The goal is maximum fraud caught without hurting new customers.

The Solution

Four models compete under one leakage-free protocol, all tied at PR-AUC 0.535–0.541; LightGBM+SMOTE is chosen, with a cost-sensitive threshold decision on top.

The Value

Flags 0.53% of traffic to catch 51.6% of fraud with only 2 false positives; value-weighted fraud loss falls from $14,503 to $6,939, about 52%.

00 Executive Summary

Executive Summary

This analysis of 138,376 e-commerce first-transaction records answers three questions: how well fraud can be predicted from a new user's opening purchase, why recall is capped near 52% no matter what model runs, and why the 98.6% precision at the default threshold measures the cutoff rather than the model. The final model is LightGBM + SMOTE, tuned on PR-AUC.

Four findings

  • The signal lives in two features; everything else is noise. Signup-to-purchase interval and device-share count carry almost all the discriminative power (SHAP mean 2.52 and 2.42; everything else below 0.7). Age, sex, value, channel and country carry no usable signal.
  • Recall is capped near 52% by the data, not the model. Half of all fraud — the 739 accounts purchasing within one second of signup — is perfectly separable; the other half is nearly indistinguishable, so recall does not move with threshold or model.
  • Precision at a fixed threshold reflects score scale, not model quality. The same LightGBM with class weights ranks identically (PR-AUC 0.54) yet shows only 34% precision at 0.5.
  • Most categorical signals do not survive a significance test. Marketing source is not significant (chi-square p = 0.27), weekend signup is not (p = 0.26), and country spans only 0.44%–1.91% on sample size alone.

01 The Data

The Data & the Imbalance

The data is clean: 138,376 rows, no missing values, no duplicates. The single dominant fact is the imbalance — 1.02% fraud, roughly 1 in 97. With only 1,415 fraud examples, the model can only latch onto strong, repeated signals, which is exactly why it ends up leaning on two features.

Method · Why PR-AUC for evaluation, a range join for IP

Accuracy is useless. Predicting "legitimate" for everyone scores 98.98% and catches zero fraud, so evaluation uses precision, recall and PR-AUC, which unlike ROC-AUC is sensitive to the rare class.

Recovering country from a numeric IP. A naive per-row scan of the lookup table is O(n·m); sorting both tables and using merge_asof does the range join in one pass. 14.7% of IPs fall outside every range and are marked Unknown.

02 Exploratory Analysis

What the Exploration Says

Purchase speed is the dominant signal. Legitimate users take a median of ~60 days between signup and first purchase; fraud takes a median of one second. The 739 transactions completed within one second are 100% fraud and account for 52% of all fraud in the data. The other half is spread across normal-looking intervals — the first sign of the recall ceiling.

Device sharing is the second signal, and purchase value is not a signal at all. A device tied to a single account is almost never fraud (0.35%); once three or more accounts share a device the fraud rate is 58%. Purchase value is nearly identical across classes (mean $37.9 fraud vs $36.9 legitimate) — fraudsters deliberately transact at ordinary amounts.

Significance · The channel and browser stories do not hold up

Sorted by fraud rate the differences look suggestive, but the significance tests flatten them: source is not significant (p = 0.27), browser reaches p = 0.007 only on sample size and spans a negligible 0.86%–1.13%. With 1,415 fraud cases, small rate differences are easy to over-read, so each is checked before it counts.

03 Model Bake-off

Models on Equal Terms

Three model families ran through one leakage-free protocol: an 80/20 stratified split, device- and IP-sharing counts encoded by a CountEncoder fit on training folds only, SMOTE applied inside the pipeline so synthetic rows never leak into validation, and 5-fold CV scored on average precision. A fourth variant — LightGBM with scale_pos_weight and no resampling — tests whether SMOTE changes ranking.

Model PR-AUC ROC-AUC
Logistic Regression (SMOTE) 0.535 0.757
Random Forest (SMOTE) 0.541 0.762
LightGBM (SMOTE) 0.540 0.758
LightGBM (weighted) 0.541 0.763

All four land in a 0.535–0.541 band whose width is smaller than the ±0.05 fold-to-fold standard deviation — they are tied. We proceed with tuned LightGBM + SMOTE (PR-AUC 0.535 on the held-out test set), while noting that logistic regression would be a defensible, simpler choice.

Artifact · 98.6% precision measures the threshold, not the model

The model reaches 98.6% precision at threshold 0.5, but the same LightGBM trained with class weights (scale_pos_weight) instead of SMOTE ranks identically (PR-AUC 0.54) yet shows 34% precision at 0.5 — because it places scores on a different scale (mean predicted probability 0.125 versus the SMOTE model's 0.010). Precision at a fixed cutoff reflects where the threshold sits on a bimodal score distribution, not which model is better.

04 The Recall Ceiling

The Recall Ceiling

The precision-recall curve holds precision at ~1.0 up to recall 0.52, then falls off a cliff. Sweeping the threshold shows recall flat at ~0.52 throughout while precision climbs — the threshold trades precision, not recall. The score histogram explains why: predictions pile up near 0 or near 1, with almost nothing between. The fraud near 1.0 is the instant-purchase half, trivially caught; the other half scores as low as legitimate traffic, so no threshold recovers it.

This is the honest limit of the feature set. On the test set the tuned model catches 146 of 283 fraud cases (51.6%) with 2 false positives out of 27,393 legitimate transactions (confusion matrix TN 27,391 / FP 2 / FN 137 / TP 146).

Data caveat · Live recall would likely be lower

The dataset is constructed so that instant-purchase and device-sharing patterns are unusually clean, which is why the top two features are so dominant and the instant-purchase cluster is 100% fraud. On live traffic these signals would be noisier, so real-world recall would likely be lower than 52%, not higher.

05 Cost & Threshold

Cost and the Threshold Decision

The 0.5 threshold is not a business choice. Pricing the two errors makes the trade-off explicit: a false negative costs the lost goods plus a flat chargeback fee, computed per transaction from its real purchase value; a false positive costs a fixed penalty for blocking a good first-time customer, whose true value is a business assumption rather than a measured number, so it is swept.

False-positive penalty Optimal threshold Total cost vs. approve all
$50 0.64 $6,939 −52%
$100 0.64 $6,939 −52%
$200 0.64 $6,939 −52%

Because the scores are bimodal, the optimal threshold is insensitive to the exact penalty — anywhere from $50 to $200 lands near 0.6, and value-weighted fraud loss ($14,503 → $6,939) falls ~52% versus approving everyone (the model catches the instant-purchase half, which carries about half the total fraud value). A very high false-positive cost pushes the threshold up toward the pure instant-purchase cluster (recall ~0.52, essentially zero false alarms); a low false-positive cost pulls it down to trade precision for a few more catches. With this data the flat optimum lets the business prioritize precision at little cost in recall.

06 What the Model Uses

What the Model Keys On

SHAP over the test set confirms the two-feature story in plain terms: a short gap between signup and purchase is the strongest push toward "fraud" (real customers browse and return; automated or stolen-credential purchases happen in seconds), and a device shared by many accounts is the second (the fraud-ring pattern). Everything else — sex, weekend, browser, hour — contributes a small fraction of the top two's weight and is better read as minor correlation than a real driver. The model ignores age, purchase value and marketing source.

07 Productionizing

Turning Scores into Product Decisions

Binning the final model's test-set scores shows what a tiering actually looks like:

Tier Volume Fraud rate Fraud caught
High (≥ 0.7): decline + manual review 0.53% 100% 146
Medium (0.3–0.7): step-up verification 0.01% 0% 0
Low (< 0.3): auto-approve 99.5% 0.5% 137
Insight · The middle tier is nearly empty

The high tier — 0.53% of traffic, essentially all fraud — is declined in real time and routed to manual review, catching ~52% of fraud with almost no false positives. The low tier is auto-approved with no friction; it is also where the missed half of fraud hides. The medium tier is nearly empty: the model does not produce a meaningful "uncertain" population at 0.5–0.9, so a step-up-verification lane built on these scores would sit idle. Recovering more fraud means deliberately lowering the high-tier threshold into the low-precision region, where each extra catch costs rapidly rising false positives.

Before deployment

  • Validate the sharing-count features with a time-ordered backtest (they currently assume full-history knowledge).
  • Monitor precision and recall on live traffic against the semi-synthetic clean split.
  • Roll out behind an A/B test on a small traffic slice, tracking fraud caught, false-alarm rate and first-purchase conversion.
