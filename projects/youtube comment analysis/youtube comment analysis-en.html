The Challenge

5.82M comments, no ground-truth label at all. "Train a classifier and report the AUC" is circular reasoning — the task needs a method that honestly estimates the real signal, not an inflated score.

The Solution

Weak-supervision labels + feature decoupling + grouping by user + 300 hand labels + an ablation that quantifies leakage, separating what merely reconstructs the rules from the real signal.

The Value

Isolates 44,623 high-confidence owners at ~0.94 precision (91,443, 3.6%, at τ_F1); creators are ranked by likely-owner audience share to guide KOL targeting (Zak George 0.51 top).

00 Executive Summary

Executive Summary

There is no ground-truth owner label in this dataset. Written honestly as weak supervision, held out by user, and checked against 300 hand-labelled comments, the analysis estimates how much genuine ownership signal the comments carry. The answer is a modest but real signal, reported with intervals rather than a single headline number.

Four findings

  • A high agreement score is mostly rule reconstruction. With the rule's own words left in the vocabulary a model reaches AUC 0.96; remove those words and it falls to 0.91. That 0.05 is not skill, it is the model re-deriving the regexes it was trained to imitate.
  • There is real signal underneath. Even blind to the trigger words, the masked model reaches 0.91 agreement-AUC by leaning on comment length, care vocabulary, creator and user behaviour, and language — not on "dog" or "cat".
  • The model finds owners the rules miss. In comment bands where the rules never fire, the model's high-scored picks are 3–9× more likely to be true owners than the band's base rate, and it out-predicts the full-vocabulary model there. That is generalisation, not memorisation.
  • Honesty costs precision points, not the whole result. A precision-first threshold still isolates ~45k users at ~0.94 precision.

Method · Why not just report 0.96

The label is synthesized by three regexes, and the same content words can enter the model — if those words are left in the vocabulary the model naturally reaches 0.96 agreement-AUC, but that is mostly reconstructing the labelling rule itself. This analysis defines the gap between 0.96 and the masked 0.91 explicitly as "rule reconstruction" and subtracts it, reporting only the 0.91 as genuine generalisation signal.

01 The Corpus

The Data

The dataset is a scrape of comments under pet-focused YouTube channels: three columns (creator_name, userid, comment), 5.82M rows after dropping 491 empty comments. It is not a panel of user behaviour: two thirds of users comment exactly once, 85% appear under a single creator, and the top 38 creators hold 66% of all comments — a creator Gini of 0.94.

02 Weak Supervision

Weak Supervision, Not a Hidden Truth Label

Three labelling functions write the noisy label: a strong possession rule ("my dog", "we adopted a kitten", "I'm a dog mom"), a weaker context rule ("my vet", "his crate", "when I feed my …"), and a negation rule ("I want a dog", "my friend's cat", "don't have a pet"). A comment is labelled 1 when a positive rule fires and negation does not. The result is a 1.5% positive rate.

Design · Care vocabulary is deliberately kept out of the label

The care vocabulary is deliberately kept out of the label, so it can serve as a legitimate feature later. These labels are rules, not truth, and the negatives are unlabelled, not confirmed non-owners — which is why the evaluation below leans on human labels, not on the rule's own recall.

03 Decoupling & Ablation

Decoupling, and the Ablation that Measures the Leakage

The model is trained on features chosen to not re-encode the rule: comment-level text style, the care-term count, and behaviour-only aggregates of the user and creator (counts, lengths, breadth, rank). User- and creator-level "ownership rate" features — which for the 67% single-comment users are just that comment's label — are dropped. Aggregates are computed once over the whole corpus and shared by training and scoring, so there is no train/serve skew; the split is by userid, so no user sits on both sides.

The TF-IDF vocabulary for the main model removes every content word the rules key on. A second, ablation model keeps them. On held-out users the masked model scores 0.91 and the full-vocabulary one 0.96 — the gap is the rule reconstruction.

04 Human Validation

Human Validation is the Honest Headline

300 comments were sampled across five strata — where each rule fires, where the care words fire without a possession phrase, and where nothing fires at all — and labelled by hand (LLM-assisted, spot-checked, 0 undecidable). The strong-rule stratum is 93% true owners; the weaker rule only 42%; the no-signal base rate is 5.6%.

Decisive test · Rule-silent band: generalisation, not memorisation

The decisive test is the ablation on the rule-silent band: there the masked model beats the full-vocabulary one (AUC 0.70 vs 0.61). The full-vocabulary model wins only where the rules already fire — that is, it wins at reconstructing the rule and loses at finding the owners the rule missed. Among comments the rules never tagged, a human confirmed cases like "i remembered my doggy baby named denver" and "i have a golden retriever and it was so hard to train him" — ownership the regexes had no pattern for.

05 What the Model Uses

What the Model Keys On

SHAP on the masked model puts comment length first, then language fragments (non-English tokens push the score down, consistent with the English-only scope), care_term_count, and creator/user behaviour. No animal noun appears, because the model cannot see them — which is the point.

06 Full-corpus Scoring

Scoring the Whole Corpus, and Who the Owners Are

All 5.82M comments are scored through the identical pipeline; the held-out train/serve score difference is exactly zero. Two operating points are reported: 91,443 likely-owner users (3.6%) at the F1 threshold, 44,623 (1.8%) at the precision-first threshold.

Ranked by predicted likely-owner share of audience, with a 1,000-user minimum so a tiny channel cannot top the list, the leaders are training and small-pet channels — Zak George's Dog Training (0.51), StormyRabbits and Lennon The Bunny (~0.44) — which fits: their audiences skew toward people who own the animal.

07 Limits & Reproducibility

Limits & Reproducibility

Limitations

The training labels are rules, not truth; the negatives are unlabelled positives, so recall against the rules is not real recall.

The validation set is 300 comments and LLM-assisted; its intervals are wide, and the labeller shares some language intuition with the rules — the rule-silent strata that carry the generalisation claim are least affected by that overlap.

Everything is English-only (~94% of comments) and comment-level. A comment is weak evidence of ownership, and ownership is not consumption; the dataset is a creator-comment scrape, so "user behaviour" reflects collection, not browsing.

Reproducibility

One seed (42) throughout; GroupShuffleSplit and StratifiedGroupKFold by userid; aggregates, scores, and the validation sample cached under artifacts/, model, vectorizer and feature spec under models/. Run end-to-end in about 29 minutes (LF scan ~75s, hyper-parameter search ~17 min, full scoring ~2 min):

uv run jupyter nbconvert --to notebook --execute --inplace "YouTube Comments Analysis.ipynb"
